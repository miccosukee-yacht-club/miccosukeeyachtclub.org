Captain Rod's sloop wintered in Tarpon Springs and required delivery to her berth in Carrabelle. The Commodore and Captain Mack signed on as crew. The plan was straightforward on paper: depart Palm Harbour, lay a rhumb line of roughly 325 degrees true, and put in at Carrabelle some thirty hours later, give or take the weather.

The weather, as the weather does, took.

## Departure

We cleared the channel at evening and stood out under power. The Gulf was glassy, the sky cloudless, and the stars came on in the order they are supposed to. For several hours the only sounds were the diesel at cruise and the occasional comment on the brightness of Orion. Captain Mack made coffee. The Commodore plotted positions. Captain Rod took the first watch.

At one point in the small hours, somewhere west of the Steinhatchee approach, Mack and the Commodore heard whales. There is no other word for the sound, and no reason for either of us to have invented it. The chart plotter showed nothing. The hydrophones in our heads were sufficient.

## Of the Record

In the morning watch the wind filled in from the southeast at fifteen knots, with gusts to twenty. The Commodore was at the helm. The sails were trimmed without committee. The log recorded 4.7 knots through the water on sail alone, which stands as the Club's offshore speed record under canvas and shall remain so until a member sees fit to break it.

The record was witnessed by two crew, entered into the log in pen, and is not subject to recount.

## Of the Weather

The second day was not the first day. The clouds came up out of the west and brought with them rain, then more rain, then a sea state that the National Weather Service would describe as three to five feet and that the crew would describe in other terms. Captain Rod was struck first. The Commodore followed within the hour. Both retired below in a condition unbecoming of officers but unavoidable under the circumstances.

Captain Mack stood the deck.

For many hours, through rain and confused seas and the particular kind of cold that finds its way past foul-weather gear at the cuffs, Fairy Mack manned the cockpit alone. He held the course. He held the boat. He held, in due course, the entire reputation of the Club's offshore crew. The Commodore acknowledges the debt formally and on the record.

## Landfall

Carrabelle came up on schedule. The harbor entrance was made in daylight. The crew, restored to function by the smell of land, took the lines. The sloop was made fast. Coffee was served ashore, and was excellent.

The log was closed in good order.
