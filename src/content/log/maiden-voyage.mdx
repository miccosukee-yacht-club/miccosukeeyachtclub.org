The morning of the fourteenth presented conditions that one might charitably describe as acceptable: a light chop from the southwest, visibility to the opposite shore, and a barometric pressure holding steady at 1013 millibars. The Club's meteorological officer — a role currently held by the Commodore's neighbor's weather station — offered no objection to departure.

The *M.V. Porkchop Express* was trailered to the Lake Talquin public ramp and readied at 0900 hours. Provisions were loaded in the following order: beverages first, then foodstuffs, then, at some length, the life jackets, which had been placed at the bottom of the dock locker in an optimistic arrangement that will not be repeated.

A note on geography: the Club takes its name from the Miccosukee Land Cooperative, a 344-acre intentional community in northeastern Leon County where several members reside. The Coop sits beside a lake, also called Miccosukee, which is shallow, heavily vegetated, and suitable for kayaks, herons, and quiet contemplation. It is not suitable for a pontoon barge drawing 24 inches. The Club has made its peace with this and operates accordingly.

## Observations Recorded

The Mercury 150 started on the first attempt, which was noted in the log as a favorable omen. The trim tabs required a brief adjustment — she was running two degrees down at the bow, a matter remedied before we cleared the no-wake zone.

Lake Talquin was in fine form. A great blue heron occupied the starboard channel marker and showed no inclination to yield. We gave him the wider berth.

Lunch was taken at anchor in a quiet cove off the main basin. The Club's provisions officer (also the Commodore) had prepared sandwiches of adequate but not exceptional quality. The beer was excellent. The cigars were outstanding. The stories improved as the afternoon wore on.

## Return to the Ramp

We made fast at 1630 hours. The engine was flushed per standing protocol — refer to the maintenance log, entry 002, for the full procedure. Total distance covered: eleven nautical miles by GPS, which is approximately eight and a half statute miles, or more than enough for a founding voyage.

The log was closed in good order.
